================================================================================
RESEARCH SUMMARY (read once before the demo — do NOT recite this)
================================================================================

State of the industry, May 2026:

  - The LLM-eval space has converged on three OSS frameworks:
      * DeepEval (Confident AI)  — pytest-style, 50+ metrics, code-first
      * Ragas                    — RAG-specific (faithfulness, context recall)
      * Promptfoo                — CLI / YAML, great for prompt + model A/B
    Source: Inference.net, Braintrust, genai.qa comparison guides (2026)

  - The platforms layered on top are:
      * Braintrust, LangSmith, Arize, Langfuse, Confident AI
    They add dashboards, dataset versioning, human review, online evals.

  - Industry consensus (2026): teams use TWO tools — one OSS framework for
    CI gating + one platform for review and monitoring. (Braintrust, ZenML,
    Confident AI all say this in their own words.)

  - The accepted core metrics for a RAG chatbot are:
      * Faithfulness / Groundedness   — answer supported by retrieved context
      * Answer Relevancy              — answer addresses the question
      * Contextual Precision / Recall — retriever brought back the right docs
      * Hallucination                 — claims unsupported by source
      * Toxicity / Bias / PII         — safety guardrails
    Source: Ragas, DeepEval, Patronus AI, Comet docs

  - Known DeepEval gaps in enterprise use:
      * No UI, no collaboration — QA / PM can't drive it
      * Heavy LLM-judge cost and latency at scale
      * No first-class place to put client-specific guidelines or rules
      * Multi-turn conversation eval is awkward
    Source: ZenML "DeepEval alternatives", Confident AI's own enterprise page

This is the soil we plant our pitch in. We are NOT trashing DeepEval. We're
saying: it's good, we used it, and we needed something tighter for our flow.


================================================================================
PART 1 — FRAMING  (≈ 3 MIN, SPOKEN, NO SCREEN)
================================================================================

*Stand up, close the laptop lid halfway if you can. Eye contact.*

"Before I show you the tool, I want to spend two minutes on how the industry
 actually tests chatbots today, because it's the reason we built this."

"Broadly there are three things people do."

"One — manual QA. Someone from the business pastes the bot's answers into a
 spreadsheet and gives them a thumbs up or down. This is still how most
 companies do it. It doesn't scale, two reviewers disagree, and there's no
 audit trail when a client asks 'why did you sign off on this response'."

"Two — open-source evaluation frameworks. The big three right now are
 DeepEval, Ragas, and Promptfoo. DeepEval is pytest for LLMs, fifty-plus
 built-in metrics. Ragas is laser-focused on RAG pipelines — faithfulness,
 context recall, that family of metrics. Promptfoo is the one you reach for
 when you want to A/B two prompts or two models. These are all good. They
 are all code-first."

"Three — the platforms on top: Braintrust, LangSmith, Arize, Langfuse,
 Confident AI. These add the UI, the dataset versioning, the dashboards.
 Most serious teams in 2026 are running one framework plus one platform."

*Pause — call out the observability category specifically, because someone
 in the room has heard of these and will ask.*

"Two of those names deserve a second mention because they're a different
 category — observability. Langfuse and Arize Phoenix are the open-source
 leaders here. They're not really evaluation tools, they're production
 tracing tools. Every conversation the bot has in production gets traced,
 every prompt, every retrieval, every token. Then evaluators run on top of
 those traces, either online — scored live — or offline on samples."

"Langfuse is OpenTelemetry-native, fully open-source, self-hostable on
 Postgres and ClickHouse. It's the default pick when you want to own your
 data. Arize Phoenix is the eval-rigor pick — drift detection, embedding
 analysis, the ML-grade primitives Arize had before LLMs were a thing.
 LangSmith is the LangChain-native option."

"These tools are excellent for what they do — watching what the bot does
 in production. They are NOT what we needed. They answer the question
 'what happened in production yesterday.' We needed to answer 'should we
 ship this bot to the client tomorrow.' That's pre-release evaluation,
 against the client's docs, with the client's rules. Different job."

"Where they meet EvalBot is downstream — once a bot is live, the client
 plugs Langfuse or Arize into their stack to watch it run, and pipes
 flagged traces back into EvalBot as new test cases. They're complements,
 not competitors."

*Pause.*

"We started with DeepEval. We're not here to bash it — it's a solid
 framework and we still recommend it for engineering teams. But for our
 flow, three things hurt."

"First, it's code-first. Every new metric, every new rule a client gives us,
 is a Python pull request. Our QA team had to wait on engineering for every
 single change. That broke the flow."

"Second, the judge has no idea what 'correct' means for our client. DeepEval
 will tell you a bot's answer is faithful — but faithful to what? Out of the
 box, it judges against its own world knowledge. For a domain chatbot —
 banking, healthcare, internal HR — that's the wrong reference. We need the
 judge grounded in the client's own documents, every time."

"Third, there's no first-class place for the client's guidelines. Every
 client gives us a rulebook — tone, escalation language, disclaimers,
 things the bot must never say. In DeepEval those rules live in code as
 custom metrics. They should live as plain English next to the bot."

*Beat.*

"So we built EvalBot. It's not a DeepEval replacement for the world. It's
 our internal harness, designed around the way Alphabin actually tests bots
 for clients. Three principles."

"One — every score is grounded in the client's own knowledge base. The
 judge doesn't get to invent the right answer. The right answer is
 retrieved from the client's docs."

"Two — guidelines live as markdown. QA writes them, the evaluator reads
 them, the report quotes them. No code in the loop."

"Three — hybrid scoring. We pair deterministic ML and NLP metrics with the
 LLM judge. When a client asks us to defend a score, we can show both
 numbers and explain when they agree and when they don't."

*Pause — this is the question someone in the room is about to ask.*

"Quick word on WHY we use an LLM as the judge in the first place, because
 it's the obvious question. There are basically four ways to score a
 chatbot response, and we use three of them."

"Option one — human evaluation. SMEs read every response and grade it.
 This is the gold standard. It's also slow, expensive, and inconsistent —
 two reviewers disagree about thirty percent of the time on the same
 response. We can't ship a client a bot by asking humans to read ten
 thousand answers."

"Option two — classic NLP metrics. BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR. These compare
 word overlap with a reference answer. They were designed for machine
 translation in 2002. For chatbots they're almost useless — if the bot
 says 'we're open from nine to five' and the reference says 'business
 hours are 9am to 5pm,' BLEU scores it low because the words don't match.
 Same meaning, wrong score."

"Option three — embedding-based metrics. BERTScore, semantic similarity.
 Better than BLEU because they compare meaning, not words. We DO use
 these — they're part of our deterministic ML score. But they can't tell
 you WHY something is wrong. They give you a number, not a reason. A
 client report that says 'the bot scored 0.71 on BERTScore' doesn't help
 anyone."

"Option four — LLM as a judge. You give another LLM the question, the
 bot's answer, the reference, and the rules, and ask it to score and
 explain. The data on this is now well-established: GPT-4 class judges
 agree with human reviewers about eighty percent of the time, which is
 roughly the level humans agree with each other. And critically, the
 judge writes a sentence of justification next to every score. That
 sentence is what goes into the client report."

"So our stack is: embeddings and NLP for the deterministic floor, LLM
 judge for reasoning and explanation, humans in the loop only for the
 cases where the two disagree. That last part is the scatter plot I'll
 show you in a few minutes."

"That's why LLM-as-judge. Let me show you the tool."

*Open the laptop. Switch to the browser. EvalBot project page is already
 loaded.*

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 1 — THE PROJECT  (≈ 60 SEC)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Screen: the project page for a pre-loaded demo bot. KB documents listed on
 one side, guidelines markdown on the other.]

"Each bot we test is a project. Two things go into it."

"On the left, the knowledge base. These are the same documents the client's
 bot is built on. PDF, Word, markdown, scraped web pages — whatever the
 source is. EvalBot indexes them into a local vector store. Every score the
 judge gives later is grounded against this corpus."

"On the right, the guidelines file. Plain markdown. This one says things
 like 'always include business hours when discussing availability,'
 'never disclose internal account IDs,' 'if the user mentions self-harm,
 escalate immediately.' QA writes this directly. No code."

"That's the setup. From here, every evaluation answers one question:
 given THIS knowledge base and THESE rules, how did the bot do?"


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 2 — A SINGLE EVALUATION  (≈ 3 MIN)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Click "New evaluation". Paste a pre-prepared question and a pre-prepared
 bot response from your notes file.]

"Here's a real question a user asked the bot, and here's the response the
 bot gave. I'm going to evaluate it."

[Click Run. While it runs, keep talking.]

"Behind the scenes, three things are happening in parallel."

"One — we run ML and NLP metrics. Semantic similarity using a local
 embedding model, lexical overlap, readability, completeness against the
 retrieved context. Deterministic. Same input, same score, every time.
 No LLM cost. This is our floor."

"Two — we retrieve the relevant chunks from the client's docs and ask the
 LLM judge to write the answer the bot *should* have given. We call this
 the reference answer. It's grounded — the judge isn't guessing, it's
 reading the client's own documents."

"Three — the judge scores the bot's answer against that reference, on five
 dimensions: similarity, accuracy, completeness, relevance, readability.
 And in parallel, it reads the guidelines markdown and flags any
 violations."

[Result appears on screen.]

"OK — score is 6.8 out of 10. Let's read it."

[Click into the score breakdown.]

"ML score: 7.1. Judge score: 6.5. They mostly agree. When they disagree
 sharply, that's a flag — usually means the bot wrote something fluent but
 wrong, and the judge caught it."

[Click the reference answer.]

"Here's what the bot should have said, generated from these specific
 chunks of the knowledge base — you can see the citations. This is the
 thing DeepEval doesn't give us out of the box. The judge is grounded in
 the client's world, not its own."

[Click into the per-dimension breakdown.]

"For each dimension, the judge gives a score AND a written explanation.
 This explanation goes straight into the client report. We don't have to
 invent it."

[Click guideline findings.]

"And here — the bot violated this rule from the guidelines file. The
 system quotes the offending sentence and cites the rule. That's not
 something a generic metric gives you. That's specific to this client."

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Paste a second prepared bot response that contains a fake email or an SSN.]

"Let me show you what happens when the bot leaks something it shouldn't."

[Click Run.]

[Red banner appears.]

"PII detection runs on every response. Pattern-based, instant, zero LLM
 cost. Emails, phone numbers, SSNs, credit cards. If you're a regulated

 team asks about. We catch it before the client does."

STEP 4 — CUSTOM CHECK  (≈ 1 MIN)  ★ the moment for QA
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

*This is the most important 60 seconds in the demo. Slow down.*

[Click "Add custom check". Type the sentence in front of them.]

"Watch this. I'm going to add a new rule, live, right now. No code, no
 deploy."

[Type: "The response must mention business hours whenever the user
 asks about availability."]

[Save. Re-run the evaluation against the same response.]

"The custom check is in the next evaluation. The bot didn't mention
 business hours, so it fails — and the judge tells me exactly why."

"This is the moment that decided it for us. In DeepEval, that rule is a
 Python class, a code review, a deploy. Here, QA writes the sentence and
 it's live. When a client emails us at 5pm saying 'oh by the way the bot
 should never recommend competitor products,' we add it during the call."


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 5 — BATCH / TEST SUITE  (≈ 2 MIN)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Navigate to the test suite page. Show the seed question library.]

"One evaluation is nice. The reason we built this is regression testing."

"We ship with a seed library of about a hundred questions across four risk
 categories — security, harmfulness, fact-check, hallucination. These are
 the categories that scare clients."

[Click Run suite. Heatmap starts populating.]

"This is how we sign off a bot before release. We run the whole suite,
 we get a heatmap. Green is fine. Yellow is review. Red is block-release."

[Heatmap fills.]

"Two red cells — both in the hallucination column. Let's look."

[Click into one red cell.]

"The bot invented a refund policy that isn't in the documents. Judge
 caught it, quoted the bot's claim, cited the absence in the KB. That's
 a deploy blocker."

"Every time the client updates the KB or we tune the bot, we rerun this
 suite. If a fix makes hallucination worse somewhere else, we see it
 here, not in production."


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STEP 6 — ANALYTICS  (≈ 1 MIN)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Open the analytics dashboard.]

"Last thing. This is what ships to the client."

[Point to the ML-vs-AI scatter.]

"Every dot is one evaluation. X-axis is the deterministic score, Y-axis is

 worth a human look. This single chart is how we triage which responses
 need review."

[Point to the dimension performance bars.]

"Per-dimension performance over the whole run. We can tell a client:
 'your bot is great at relevance, weak on completeness, here's where to
 invest.'"

[Click CSV export.]

"And it all exports. The client gets a CSV, a dashboard link, and every
 number is traceable back to a specific document and a specific rule."

================================================================================
ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS — PREPARED ANSWERS
================================================================================

Q: "Why not just use Confident AI's hosted Deepeval / Braintrust / LangSmith?"
A: "Three reasons. One, client data — several of our clients won't send
    chat logs to a third-party SaaS. EvalBot runs entirely local with
    Ollama if needed. Two, cost — at our volume the hosted platforms get
    expensive fast. Three, we needed the guideline-as-markdown abstraction
    and none of them have it natively. We will plug into Braintrust for
    teams that want it; the data model lines up."

Q: "How does this overlap with Langfuse / Arize Phoenix / LangSmith?"
A: "It doesn't, really — different stage of the lifecycle. Those are
    production observability tools: trace every conversation, watch drift,
    alert on anomalies in live traffic. EvalBot is pre-release evaluation:
    does this bot meet the bar before it ships? In a mature setup you'd
    run both — Langfuse or Arize in production for traces, EvalBot before
    releases for grounded scoring against the client's docs. We're
    planning a connector so flagged production traces flow back into
    EvalBot as new test cases. They're complements."

Q: "Why an LLM judge and not just BERTScore or human review?"
A: "Different jobs. BERTScore gives a number with no reasoning — fine as
    a floor, useless in a client report. Human review is the gold standard
    but doesn't scale past a few hundred responses. The LLM judge sits
    between them: it agrees with humans ~80% of the time (GPT-4 class on
    public benchmarks), it scales to thousands of evaluations, and it
    writes the justification sentence we paste into reports. We still use
    BERTScore — it's part of our deterministic ML floor — and humans
    review the cases where the ML score and judge score disagree."

Q: "How do you know the judge itself isn't hallucinating?"
A: "Two safeguards. First, the judge is grounded — it scores against a
    reference answer that came from the client's documents, not from its
    head. Second, we run the deterministic ML metrics alongside. When the
    two disagree by more than a threshold we flag for human review. The
    scatter plot you saw is exactly that triage tool."

Q: "What about multi-turn conversations?"
A: "Today we evaluate response-by-response and at the conversation level
    with a summary judge pass. Full multi-turn evaluation — coherence
    across ten turns, goal completion — is in the roadmap doc. Honestly,
    no one in the OSS space has nailed this yet."

Q: "How long to onboard a new client bot?"
A: "If they give us their KB and a guidelines doc, a couple of hours to
    a working harness. Compare that to writing custom DeepEval metrics
    per client, which was easily a week."

Q: "Which LLM is the judge?"
A: "Configurable per project. Default is Claude Sonnet 4.6 for the right
    cost-quality balance, Opus 4.7 when accuracy matters more than cost,
    GPT-4o or Gemini for clients on those stacks, Ollama for fully local."

Q: "How does this compare with Ragas specifically?"
A: "Ragas gives us the metric vocabulary — faithfulness, context recall —
    and we're aligned on those definitions. What Ragas doesn't give is
    the workflow: guidelines as markdown, custom checks from the UI, PII
    layer, the analytics. We could swap our judge engine for Ragas under
    the hood; we chose to build it to control cost and grounding."

================================================================================
FALLBACKS WHEN SOMETHING BREAKS
================================================================================

  Judge API times out
    → Switch provider in the project settings (Anthropic ↔ Gemini ↔ OpenAI)
    → Re-run. Move on.

  Evaluation is slow
    → Keep talking about grounding and guidelines. Do NOT stare at
      the spinner.

  Whole evaluation fails
    → Open a previously-run evaluation from history. Walk through it
      with the same script. Most of the audience won't know the
      difference.

  Custom check doesn't trigger
    → Show the JSON of the saved check to prove it's there, then move on.

  Heatmap empty (batch run fails)
    → Have a screenshot of a prior batch run ready. Show that.

  Out of time
    → Cut Step 6 (analytics) first.
    → Then Step 5 (batch).
    → Single eval + custom check is the MUST-SHOW core. Don't lose those.
